# Designing motion control firmware: a comprehensive survey of 3D printer and CNC architectures

The motion-control firmware landscape in 2026 has consolidated around four architectural patterns — monolithic-MCU (Marlin, GRBL, RepRapFirmware), host/MCU split with iterative step computation (Klipper, Redeem, Buildbotics), PC-plus-real-time-Linux with FPGA offload (LinuxCNC), and PC-plus-external-motion-controller (Mach4, UCCNC). **The single most important architectural insight from twenty years of evolution is that the apparent divide between 3D printer and CNC firmware is an artifact of historical regimes, not a structural necessity**: g2core, RepRapFirmware, and Klipper all demonstrate that the same engine can drive FDM, milling, laser, and plasma if work-coordinate systems, real-time overrides, spindle synchronization, and pressure-advance-shaped extrusion are designed in from the start. The firmwares that aged best (LinuxCNC's HAL, GRBL's junction deviation, Klipper's iterative solver, FluidNC's YAML config, RepRapFirmware's object model) are those that abstracted *something* — pin/signal routing, cornering physics, kinematics math, configuration, or state introspection — instead of optimizing for whatever MCU was cheap that year. The firmwares that aged worst (Marlin's monolithic Configuration.h, Mach3's parallel-port realtime, classic GRBL's AVR-only stance) all share the failure mode of letting hardware constraints leak into long-lived API decisions. This report synthesizes evidence from twenty-plus firmwares to inform from-scratch architectural choices, organized by family, then by cross-cutting algorithmic concerns, and ending with a comparative matrix and design-decision framework.

## The Marlin family and the AVR legacy

**Marlin remains the world's most-deployed 3D printer firmware** despite originating in 2011 around the 8-bit ATmega2560. Its architecture is a single-MCU C++ cooperative super-loop in `MarlinCore.cpp::loop()`, surrounded by a small set of high-priority ISRs: `Stepper::isr()` for step pulse generation, `Temperature::isr()` at ~1 kHz for heaters, plus serial and watchdog. The codebase is organized around `module/planner.cpp` (block ring buffer, two-pass forward/reverse-pass smoothing), `module/stepper.cpp` (Bresenham step generation, optional Bezier S-curve, LIN_ADVANCE, FT_MOTION dispatch), and `module/motion.cpp` (high-level moves and kinematic dispatch). Marlin 2.0 (December 2019) introduced an HAL supporting AVR, STM32 F1/F4/F7/H7, LPC176x, SAMD51, ESP32, Teensy 3.x/4.x, and HC32 — chosen at PlatformIO build time.

The motion planner is a Grbl-derivative two-pass algorithm operating in velocity-squared space (to avoid sqrt) over a `block_t` ring buffer. Default `BLOCK_BUFFER_SIZE` is 16 on AVR, 32 on 32-bit boards (8 with `DIRECT_STEPPING`). Cornering uses either classic per-axis jerk (a misnomer — really an instantaneous Δv limit) or `JUNCTION_DEVIATION` (Sonny Jeon's centripetal-acceleration model imported in PR #10650). With `S_CURVE_ACCELERATION` enabled, Marlin replaces the classic Taylor-series stepper-rate ramp with a 5th-order Bezier evaluation in hand-optimized AVR assembly — but this only modifies *step timing* during accel/decel phases, not the planner's velocity setpoints themselves. The Bezier approach is a pragmatic compromise that delivers continuous-jerk steps without rebuilding the planner.

**Step generation is Bresenham across N axes**, with the longest-axis count as the master and per-axis error counters firing follower steps on rollover. AVR step rates cap at roughly 10 kHz/axis without multi-stepping, ~40 kHz aggregate with adaptive multi-stepping (`MULTISTEPPING_LIMIT` default 128, in powers of two). STM32F4 builds reach 100–200 kHz comfortably; STM32H7 and Teensy 4 can exceed that but rarely need to. The well-known Bresenham weakness — that follower axes have time-quantized step pulses while the dominant axis sees perfect timing — produces audible aliasing at high microstepping, which is one reason Marlin recommends 16× hardware microstepping with TMC interpolation rather than 256× logical microstepping.

**Linear Advance** (Sebastian Popp's contribution, and Marlin's signature extruder feature) computes a constant velocity offset `e_advance_speed = K · e_D_ratio · acceleration · steps_per_mm_E` per block; in v1.5+ the extruder runs as a separate fixed-rate pulse train overlaid on the Bresenham E count during accel/decel. **Crucially, Marlin does not lookahead-shift extrusion across move boundaries** — only within accel/decel windows — and is documented to conflict with `S_CURVE_ACCELERATION` (issue #14728). Input shaping arrived in Marlin 2.1+ as ZV (Zero-Vibration), with the more capable **FT_MOTION** fixed-time trajectory generator merged via PR #26848 (Ulendo.io contribution). FT_MOTION is conceptually similar to Klipper's step-compression-plus-IS — it produces a buffer of "stepper events" in fixed time slices (e.g., 1 ms `FTM_TS`), each event being two bits per axis (step + dir), with selectable shapers including ZV, ZVD, ZVDD, EI, 2HEI, 3HEI, MZV, plus per-axis dynamic frequency modes that vary shaper frequency with Z height or extruded mass.

Within the Marlin family, **Prusa-Firmware-Buddy** (MK4, MK3.9, MK3.5, MK4S, MINI, XL, CORE One) is a major rewrite onto STM32F407/F427 with FreeRTOS. Tasks split between a Marlin server task (running a fork of Marlin 2.x core as a library), a GUI task, MeasurementTask, Connect task, and lwIP/USB. The XL adds STM32G0 "Dwarf" boards per tool head over RS485/Modbus. Prusa's **"Precise stepping"** — a fixed-time step grid replacing Marlin's `OCR1A` reload — predates the Ulendo FT_MOTION contribution and shares its core approach. Prusa's pressure advance is **Klipper-style with `smooth_time` convolution** rather than LIN_ADVANCE-style trapezoid editing, and the MK4's loadcell-based first-layer calibration probes the bed via a strain gauge under the heat bed (no PINDA needed). **Sailfish** (the MakerBot Replicator firmware) is technically interesting for using a **fixed-point planner** (5–6× faster than contemporary Marlin float math on AVR) but is effectively dormant. **Repetier-Firmware** is notable for double/quad stepping (an ancestor to AMASS) and "half-stepping smoothing" on the dominant axis. **Teacup** is the minimalist exemplar — pure C with a DDA rather than Bresenham, fitting in <32 KB and reaching 128 kHz on a $2 LPC1114; archived August 2023 due to GitHub 2FA enforcement.

## Klipper's host/MCU split as an architectural inflection point

Klipper deliberately abandons the all-logic-in-firmware model. The **host (Klippy)** runs on a Linux SBC in Python with hot inner loops in C via CFFI, parsing G-code, running lookahead, executing kinematic transforms, computing exact step times, and compressing them. The **MCU firmware** is a minimal real-time C program that does almost no math — it parses commands, configures peripherals, services hardware timers, and faithfully executes step pulses at clock-cycle-precise pre-computed times. Communication runs over UART, USB CDC-ACM, or CAN bus, all carrying the same self-describing binary protocol.

The split frees the host from real-time constraints because step times are computed ahead of time and queued on the MCU with hardware-timer playback. **Linux scheduling jitter is irrelevant as long as the MCU's step buffer stays fed** (typically Klipper schedules commands ~100 ms ahead). The cost of pre-computation is a buffer-underrun cliff: if the MCU's move queue empties, the printer halts mid-stroke and Klipper raises a `Timer Too Close` error. There is no graceful degradation.

**The signature innovation is the iterative step solver.** For each stepper, for each pending step, Klipper picks two initial time guesses bracketing the answer (using the previous step's time and current velocity), evaluates `kinematics(time_to_cartesian(t))` for each, and applies the secant method to converge — typically 2–4 iterations per step. The brilliance: **adding a new kinematic only requires writing the forward function**, no algebraic inversion. This is how Klipper supports 10+ kinematics (Cartesian, CoreXY, Delta, Polar, Rotary Delta, hybrid CoreXY, deltesian, generic_cartesian, etc.) with a few hundred lines of C each. Per-stepper background threads parallelize the solver across CPU cores.

**Step compression** (`stepcompress.c`) packs computed step times into `queue_step oid=… interval=u32 count=u16 add=i16` quadratic sequences, where `step[i] = step[0] + interval*i + add*i*(i-1)/2`. The bisection compressor (`compress_bisect_add`) searches the parameter space, finding the longest run of upcoming steps fitting within a per-step error window using `add` deviation bound `~11.65 * max_error / count²`. The result: typical prints generate hundreds of millions of steps but only thousands of `queue_step` commands per second cross the wire. The MCU's `stepper_event()` ISR then runs as `do_step(); next_wake = last_wake + interval; interval += add;` — pure integer adds and a hardware-timer reschedule. Klipper achieves sub-25-µs step scheduling precision and **250+ kHz per-axis step rates on STM32F4/H7 and RP2040** (reference benchmarks: ~157k steps/s aggregate on AVR; ~400k+ on LPC1768/SAM3X; well over 700k on STM32F4/RP2040).

**Input shaping is implemented as host-side convolution of the position formula consumed by the iterative solver.** Instead of asking the trapezoid queue for `position(t)`, the shaper asks for `Σ A_i · unshaped_position(t − t_i)` — and the iterative solver finds step times for the convolved formula automatically. **No MCU change, no new commands, no precomputed tables.** This elegance is the direct payoff of the iterative-solver architecture. Klipper supports ZV, MZV (Klipper-original by Dmitry Butyugin), ZVD, EI, 2HUMP_EI, and 3HUMP_EI, with auto-tuning via accelerometer (ADXL345, MPU-9250/6500/6050, LIS2DW, ICM-20948, LIS3DH) and Klipper-side FFT analysis in `scripts/calibrate_shaper.py`. **Pressure advance** uses the same trick: the extruder kinematic adds a position offset proportional to velocity, smoothed by triangular FIR convolution over `pressure_advance_smooth_time` (default 40 ms), giving continuous extruder velocity and bounded acceleration.

The communication protocol carries a self-describing RPC: commands are declared via `DECL_COMMAND(handler, "name oid=%c value=%u")`, the build collects declarations into a zlib-compressed JSON **data dictionary** embedded in the firmware, and on connect the host downloads it and binds Python wrappers to the discovered command IDs. **The protocol is self-describing per-firmware-build** — adding a command is a one-line edit. Three time bases (system, print, MCU clock) are reconciled by `clocksync.py`, which continually estimates each MCU's frequency vs. the host clock to absorb crystal drift between boards over hours-long prints. CAN bus support assigns 7-bit node IDs via `CMD_QUERY_UNASSIGNED`/`CMD_SET_KLIPPER_NODEID` admin handshakes on IDs 0x3f0/0x3f1, with USB-to-CAN bridge mode letting one MCU act as both Klipper node and bus adapter.

## RepRapFirmware: the strongest CNC features in a printer-origin firmware

**RepRapFirmware (RRF), led by David Crocker (dc42) at Duet3D**, runs C++ on Microchip ARM (SAM4E/SAM4S on Duet 2; SAME70 Cortex-M7 @ 300 MHz on Duet 3 6HC/6XD/6HD; SAME5x on Duet 3 Mini 5+; SAMC21 on toolboards). RRF 3.x runs on **FreeRTOS** with prioritized tasks (MAIN, MOVE, HEAT, NETWORK, ACCEL) so the step-pulse timer interrupt pre-empts everything except critical fault handlers. Memory is mostly static. On Duet 3 SAME70, RRF can also run in **SBC mode**: a Pi attached over SPI runs the .NET 6 Duet Software Framework (DCS, DuetWebServer, DuetPluginService) handling all G-code parsing and the object model.

RRF's motion engine is a **DDA-based ring** (default 17 entries on Duet 3) where each DDA holds per-drive DriveMovement objects on a min-heap keyed on next-step time. The ISR pops the smallest-time DM, asserts STEP for all motors due simultaneously via a single 32-bit GPIO write, recomputes `nextStepTime` analytically, and re-inserts. **Since 2015, RRF abandoned Bresenham approximation in favor of solving the closed-form motion equation analytically per axis** — and famously implemented **segment-free linear delta motion** by analytically convolving Cartesian motion with inverse delta kinematics into a quadratic-in-t tower equation. No other major open-source firmware matched this at the time. Step rates exceed 200 kHz aggregate with low jitter on SAME70, helped by M7 instruction cache and tightly-coupled-memory ISR placement. Look-ahead uses per-axis instantaneous-speed-change limits (M566 jerk) rather than junction deviation — a deliberate dc42 design choice.

**RRF has the strongest CNC feature set of any printer-origin firmware**: NIST-compliant **G53–G59.3** work coordinate systems (nine, with G10 L2/L20 and G53 single-line modifier), **tool offsets** via G10 L1 with auto-running tool-change macros (`tpre.g`, `tpost.g`, `tfree.g`), **arc support** with G2/G3/I/J/R and G17/18/19, multiple **probing types** (resistive, modulated IR, ultrasonic, switch, NC, scanning), **inline laser power encoding** (up to 8 S parameters per move for raster clustering), and **Modbus RTU/RS485 VFD support** since RRF 3.6. What's missing: rigid tapping (G84/G33.1), most canned cycles (G73, G81–G83), and full cutter compensation. Helical interpolation via G2/G3 is supported.

**Input shaping arrived in RRF 3.4** (April 2021): ZVD, ZVDD, ZVDDD, EI2, EI3, MZV, configured via M593, with shaping lengthening accel phases to N periods of the configured ringing frequency (N = 2.5 for ZVD/MZV, 3.5 for ZVDD/EI2, 4.5 for ZVDDD/EI3). RRF 3.5 synchronized extruder acceleration to the shaped XY profile so pressure advance stays in lockstep, and 3.5+ propagates input shaping into CAN-FD-connected expansion-board drivers. RRF 3.6 introduced an improved algorithm working on moves of any length — addressing a known weakness of earlier IS in very short segments. RRF deliberately **does not support different shapers per X vs Y axis** because combining them on diagonal moves would distort the toolpath; this is an explicit design choice contrasting with Klipper's smoothing approach.

**RRF's kinematic library is the broadest of any open firmware** — runtime-selectable via M669 K*n*: Cartesian, CoreXY, CoreXZ, LinearDelta, SerialSCARA, CoreXYU/UV, Hangprinter, Polar, CoreXYUV, FiveBarParallelSCARA, RotaryDelta, MarkForged, RobotKinematics (5-axis, 6-axis, hybrid via axisTypes string like `"RRPPP"`). Custom motor-mapping matrices are supported. The **Object Model** exposes the entire machine state as a JSON tree queryable via M409 or `rr_model` HTTP — slicer-injected `;customInfo key=value` headers create OM entries under `job.file.customInfo`. **Meta-G-code** (RRF 3.01+) implements indentation-based blocks with `if/elif/else`, `while` (with iterations counter, continue, break), `var` (lexically scoped) and `global` (persistent), expression evaluation with full OM access, and array/string functions — effectively a small interpreter wedged into the G-code parser. This is used in real configurations for delta auto-calibration loops that retry until σ < 0.03 mm.

**Duet 3's CAN-FD expansion bus** (1 Mbit/s arbitration, up to 8 Mbit/s data) enables distributed motion. The EXP1HCL closed-loop driver supports magnetic encoders (AS5047D/P), 5V quadrature optical (1000–2500 PPR), SPI absolute, and linear composite (Renishaw LM10IA/LM10IB linear scale + magnetic shaft encoder), tuned via M569.6 calibration maneuvers. The Motor 23CL integrates a NEMA 23 with on-board CAN-FD driver and magnetic encoder. The MB6XD provides 6 external step/dir outputs with configurable pulse width/timing via M569 T (e.g., `T5:2.5:5:7.5` = 5 µs pulse / 2.5 µs gap / 5 µs DIR setup / 7.5 µs hold).

**Smoothieware** pioneered 32-bit printer firmware on the LPC1768/9 with a bare-metal cooperative scheduler and module event system (`ON_GCODE_RECEIVED`, `ON_BLOCK_BEGIN`). It used junction deviation, Bresenham step generation at ~100 kHz, and segmented (not segment-free) delta motion. It became somewhat stagnant around 2018; **SmoothieV2** is a near-complete rewrite for STM32H745 dual-core (M7 @ 480 MHz + M4 @ 240 MHz), with FreeRTOS, junction-deviation planner, ~200 kHz step rates, and TMC2660/2590 driver support — but planned features (S-curve, M4-core step generation offload) remain unimplemented. **Redeem** runs on BeagleBone Black, with Python on the Cortex-A8 host and step generation on the on-chip 200 MHz **PRU** coprocessors (PRU0 for steps, PRU1 for endstops). Conceptually similar to Klipper's split, but the realtime processor is on-chip sharing DRAM. Niche but architecturally instructive.

## The GRBL family and the CNC reference lineage

**GRBL — by Simen Svale Skogsrud (2009) and rewritten 2011–2017 by Sungeun "Sonny" Jeon — is the single most influential motion-control codebase in the hobby world.** It fits in ~30 KB on an ATmega328P with 2 KB SRAM, with a flat single-directory architecture (`gcode.c`, `motion_control.c`, `planner.c`, `stepper.c`, `protocol.c`, `serial.c`, `system.c`, `report.c`, `settings.c`, `coolant_control.c`, `spindle_control.c`, `limits.c`, `probe.c`). Sonny Jeon effectively retired from active development after v1.1f (January 2017); the torch passed to grblHAL (Terje Io), FluidNC (Bart Dring/Mitch Bradley/Stefan de Bruijn), and grbl-Mega-5X (Gauthier Brière).

**Junction deviation — Sonny Jeon's most-cited contribution** — replaced GRBL's earlier fixed-junction-speed and Reprap-style jerk with a centripetal-acceleration-based junction velocity. At each junction, fit a circle tangent to both segments; the junction deviation `δ` (`$11`) is the perpendicular distance from the geometric corner to the circle. Geometry gives `R = δ · sin(θ/2) / (1 − sin(θ/2))`, and the centripetal limit gives `v_max² = a · δ · sin(θ/2) / (1 − sin(θ/2))`. The angle `θ` comes from `cos θ = −u₁·u₂` (one cheap dot product, no `acos`); the half-angle uses the identity `sin²(θ/2) = (1 − cos θ)/2`. **Two `sqrt()` calls per junction, zero `sin()`/`acos()`.** The deviation is purely virtual — the machine drives through the geometric junction; δ only parameterizes cornering aggression. This algorithm has been backported into Marlin, Smoothieware, Klipper (as "square corner velocity"), and effectively every modern hobby planner.

**AMASS (Adaptive Multi-Axis Step Smoothing)** is Sonny Jeon's other major contribution. At low feed rates, Bresenham aliasing produces audible groaning on slow axes. AMASS oversamples the ISR by a power of 2 (Level 1 = ×2, Level 2 = ×4, Level 3 = ×8) by bit-shifting Bresenham step-event counts left while keeping axis step counts unchanged. Non-dominant axes can pulse on any of the 2ᴺ intermediate ticks, distributing pulses uniformly in time while preserving Bresenham exactness. Powers of two are chosen because multiplications become single-cycle bit-shifts on AVR. ISR load increases (Level 3 needs ~4× ISR rate) but each ISR is "dumb"; heavy lifting happens in a segment preparation routine in main-loop context. GRBL on AVR sustains ~30 kHz/axis with this approach.

**GRBL's step pulse hardware uses a two-timer scheme**: Timer1 in CTC mode is the main step-event timer; Timer2 (8-bit) is a one-shot **step-reset timer** programmed at the start of each step pulse to fire after `$0` µs (default 10 µs) and drive the step pins low. This decouples pulse width from step interval and gives clean fixed-width pulses regardless of frequency. The streaming protocol uses character-counting for buffer management (host tracks bytes in GRBL's 128-byte RX buffer) plus real-time commands (`?`, `!`, `~`, Ctrl-X) intercepted in the serial RX ISR out-of-band — never enqueued. v1.1 added extended real-time bytes (0x80–0xFF) for safety door, jog cancel, feed/rapid/spindle overrides (`0x90` 100% feed, `0x91/0x92` ±10%, `0x93/0x94` ±1%, `0x95–0x97` rapid 100/50/25%, `0x99–0x9C` spindle overrides). **The ability for a sender to inject real-time bytes mid-stream is a long-standing CNC convention worth preserving in any new firmware.**

GRBL's **laser mode** (`$32=1`) is a signature feature: M3 holds constant power regardless of feed; **M4 dynamic power scales PWM duty by current step rate / programmed feedrate**, eliminating burnt corners on slow-accel machines because actual energy per unit length stays constant. This idea has been emulated everywhere (Marlin's Continuous/Dynamic Inline modes, FluidNC, grblHAL). Classic GRBL lacks G81–G89 canned cycles, G33/G76 threading, G96 CSS, G41/G42 cutter comp, subroutines, parameters, M6 tool change — limitations that drove the grblHAL fork.

**grblHAL (Terje Io)** is the most ambitious continuation, splitting GRBL into a processor-independent core that depends only on a `hal_t` struct of function pointers, plus per-MCU drivers (STM32 F1/F4/F7/H7, RP2040, ESP32, IMXRT1062 Teensy 4, MSP432, K64F, SAM3X8E, LPC176x — 15+ MCU families), plus plugins. Plugins for networking (Telnet/WebSocket/FTP/MQTT/HTTP), SD streaming, I²C keypad, MPG, encoder, Trinamic drivers, Modbus VFD (HuanYang/YL620/Durapulse/MODVFD), spindle sync, plasma/THC, OpenPnP, BLTouch, EEPROM/FRAM register new M-codes, new `$` settings, and event subscriptions. **A private plugin needs no changes to core or driver beyond a single `init()` call.** grblHAL adds canned cycles G73/G81–G86/G89, **G33 spindle-synchronized motion and G76 threading** (with spindle-sync plugin and encoder), G33.1 rigid tapping (experimental), **G96 CSS and G97 RPM mode**, G7/G8 lathe diameter/radius, G50/G51 axis scaling, G65 macro call, full G43 tool-table tool length, G5/G5.1 splines, M62–M65 sync digital out, M66 wait on input. On a Teensy 4.1 (IMXRT1062 @ 600 MHz), grblHAL drives sustained step rates well over 200 kHz/axis with deep lookahead.

**FluidNC** is the ESP32 successor to grbl_ESP32 with three radical ideas: **YAML configuration at runtime on a LittleFS partition** (no recompile to change pinout, axis count, kinematics, or features), **built-in web UI** (Esp32_WebUI fork over WebSocket+REST), and pluggable kinematics/spindles. Its **stepping engines** are the most innovative aspect: `Timers` (classic), `RMT` (Remote Control peripheral, originally for IR remote pulse trains, now a per-axis 64-entry RAM of 32-bit entries each encoding a 15-bit duration and 1-bit logic level — DMA-driven with sub-microsecond accuracy and zero CPU jitter), and the genuinely novel `I2S_stream`/`I2S_static`. **The I²S engine repurposes the ESP32's audio serializer to clock out a parallel data stream through external 74HC(T)595 shift registers** — DMA pulls 32-bit samples at 1 MHz BCLK, each sample becoming the simultaneous state of all output bits in the next 1 µs cycle. **It's effectively a poor-man's FPGA step generator**, supporting 6, 8, even 10 axes from a $5 ESP32 dev module plus a couple of buffer chips. The technique was first developed by Simon Jouet for an Ender 3 ESP32 driver, ported by Michiyasu Odaki into Grbl_ESP32, then evolved by Mitch Bradley into FluidNC. The trade-off: I²S latency is a few buffer frames (~1 ms slower hardware E-stop response than direct GPIO).

**grbl-Mega-5X** (Gauthier Brière) extends grbl-Mega to 5 or 6 axes on RAMPS-class hardware. With `N_AXIS=3` it produces a binary byte-identical to upstream by design. Extra axes are independent Cartesian-style (no inverse kinematics for true 5-axis simultaneous machining) — useful for indexing, dual gantries, or hot-wire foam, not genuine 5-axis CAM.

## LinuxCNC and the HAL architectural reference

**LinuxCNC (descended from NIST EMC, GPLv2) is the gold-standard open-source CNC reference architecture.** Its G-code interpreter descends directly from the **NIST RS274NGC Interpreter v3** (Kramer/Proctor/Messina, NISTIR 6556, August 2000) — the canonical specification any new G-code parser should mirror. The architecture is a five-layer stack: GUI/UI (Axis, GMOCCAPY, Touchy, QtPyVCP, QtDragon, QtPlasmaC) → task/milltask (state machine) → rs274ngc interpreter (emits canonical machining functions defined in `canon.hh`: `STRAIGHT_TRAVERSE`, `ARC_FEED`, `SET_FEED_RATE`, `SELECT_TOOL`, `CHANGE_TOOL`) → motion/motmod (trajectory planner, kinematics, per-joint PID) → HAL real-time components. IPC uses **NML (Neutral Manufacturing Language)** — the NIST RCS shared-memory channel protocol — letting any-language UIs send commands to task without compiled-in coupling.

**HAL is the architectural masterpiece.** It is "essentially a software breadboard" — functional blocks (encoder counter, step generator, PID, AND gate, scaling) wired together with named signals like a circuit. Five entities: **components** (~150+ built-in: parport, stepgen, encoder, pid, and2/or2/not, mux4, lut5, siggen, weighted_sum, motmod, hostmot2, classicladder, gs2_vfd, pyvcp, gladevcp), **pins** (typed bit/float/s32/u32 with IN/OUT/IO direction), **signals** (named wires connecting one writer to many readers), **parameters** (tunable scalars, set via `setp`), and **functions** (executable C entry points scheduled into threads via `addf`). **Threads** are real-time periodic schedulers: `base-thread` at 25–50 µs (20–50 kHz, used for software step generation, parallel-port toggling) and `servo-thread` at 1 ms (1 kHz, runs `motion-controller`, kinematics, PID, ClassicLadder).

The `halcmd` vocabulary (`loadrt`, `loadusr`, `addf`, `net`, `setp`, `sets`, `start`/`stop`, `show`, `save`) plus `halmeter`/`halscope`/`halshow` introspection tools make HAL configurations debuggable like circuits. The `.comp` preprocessor language lets new components be authored in ~10 lines of declarative pin/param/funct headers plus C in a `FUNCTION(name)` block, compiled and installed via `halcompile --install`. **Under RTAI, components were kernel modules; under RT_PREEMPT (now standard), they run in userspace `rtapi_app` with `SCHED_FIFO`** — same source code, different build flavor. Latency tuning involves `latency-test`, `isolcpus`, `irqaffinity`, `nohz_full`, disabled C-states, and disabled Intel SpeedStep. Atom/Celeron J1900 boards typically deliver RT_PREEMPT 1 ms servo at ~3.5 µs jitter and 25 µs base at ~13 µs jitter; RTAI on the same hardware gets ~2 µs / 4 µs.

**Step generation paths** range from software stegen via parallel port (legacy, ~40 kHz on RTAI, ~20 kHz on RT_PREEMPT) to **Mesa FPGA HostMot2 cards** (the gold standard): 5i25 PCI, 7i92 Ethernet, 7i76E/7i96/7i95 all-in-one Ethernet, with FPGA bitstreams composed of StepGen, QCount, PWMGen, SSerial, BISS, SSI, DPLL modules selected at `PIN_*.vhd` instantiation time and self-described via an IDROM. **Hardware Mesa stepgens reach several MHz** — the 33-bit accumulator runs at 33/50/100/200 MHz FPGA clock. Servo control via Mesa 7i77 wires `hm2.encoder.position` → `pid.feedback`, `joint.motor-pos-cmd` → `pid.command`, `pid.output` → `hm2.pwmgen.value`.

**LinuxCNC's trajectory planner** has historically been parabolic-blend with single-segment lookahead; **Robert Ellenberg's circular-blend-arc TP (merged 2014, default 2.7+) replaces parabolic blends with circular arcs** sized so deviation from the corner equals G64 P tolerance and centripetal+tangential acceleration stay within machine limits. Multi-segment lookahead (`ARC_BLEND_OPTIMIZATION_DEPTH = 50`), naive-CAM detector (G64 Q merges near-collinear segments before blending), and arc-arc/line-arc transitions. The new TP delivers ~25% runtime improvement on dense-segment toolpaths. **LinuxCNC's TP is acceleration-limited (trapezoidal accel), not jerk-limited.** Third-party integrations (scurve TP via Ruckig) exist but aren't mainline. NURBS (G5/G5.1/G5.2/G5.3) are evaluated and decomposed into G1 segments before feeding the TP — true native NURBS interpolation requires industrial controls (Siemens 840D, Fanuc 30i, Heidenhain TNC).

The **rs274ngc interpreter** implements parameters (`#1`–`#5400` numeric with reserved ranges; named `#<name>` local; `#<_name>` global), expressions in `[…]` brackets with full operator/function set including `ATAN`/`ABS`/`COS`/`EXP`/`FIX`/`LN`/`SIN`/`SQRT`/`EXISTS`, **O-codes for control flow** (`o100 sub … endsub … call`, `o100 if … elseif … else … endif`, `o100 while … endwhile`, `o100 do … while`, `o100 repeat … endrepeat`, `o100 break/continue/return`), file-based subroutines from `SUBROUTINE_PATH`, and full canned cycles (G73/G74/G76/G81/G82/G83/G84/G85/G86/G89), **G33 spindle-synchronized motion**, **G33.1 rigid tap**, **G96/G97 CSS/RPM**, plus probing G38.2–G38.5 with results in `#5061`–`#5069`. **Cutter compensation lookahead is shallow (1–2 moves)** — a documented weakness vs. industrial controls. **Tool length offsets** include G43 H<n> from tool table and G43.1 X Y Z dynamic.

**LinuxCNC's `REMAP` facility** lets users redefine G/M codes via Python prologs/epilogs or NGC O-word subroutines: `REMAP = M62 modalgroup=5 prolog=foo_prolog python=do_m62 epilog=foo_epilog`. Custom tool changers, probing wizards, plasma cycles, and alternate canned cycles are all built this way; `yield INTERP_EXECUTE_FINISH` synchronizes interpreter read-ahead with motion completion. **PlasmaC/QtPlasmaC** is the reference open-source plasma implementation, using **External Offsets** (a HAL pin that applies real-time axis position offset outside the trajectory planner — critical so THC and gcode motion don't fight) plus Mesa **THCAD** voltage-to-frequency converters reading arc voltage as encoder velocity. Velocity-anti-dive locks THC near corners; kerf-crossing detection catches voltage rate-of-change anomalies.

LinuxCNC's kinematics architecture distinguishes joint space (per-motor) from world/axis space (X, Y, Z, A, B, C, U, V, W). A kinematics module exports `kinematicsForward`, `kinematicsInverse`, and `kinematicsType` (IDENTITY/FORWARD_ONLY/INVERSE_ONLY/BOTH). Built-in modules: `trivkins` (configurable per-joint, supports gantry via `coordinates=xyyz`), `corexykins`, `genhexkins` (Stewart platform), `genserkins` (D-H parameter serial arms), `pumakins`, `scarakins`, `lineardeltakins`, `rotarydeltakins`, `5axiskins`, `xyzac-trt-kins`, `xyzbc-trt-kins`. Custom kinematics are written via `userkins.comp`. **This pluggable forward/inverse + joint/world separation is the architectural pattern any serious motion firmware should adopt.**

**Machinekit** ports LinuxCNC to BeagleBone with PRU step generation but is effectively in maintenance mode. **Mach3** runs as a Windows kernel-mode driver hooking a high-priority timer to write the parallel port at 25–45 kHz; modern Windows kernels with HPET, SMI, ACPI throttling, GPU drivers, and CPU frequency scaling make stable rates impossible above ~25 kHz. Closed-source, effectively legacy. **Mach4** is a complete rewrite delegating all real-time motion to external controllers (ESS Smooth Stepper, CSMIO/IP-S, Pokeys, Galil) via plugin API; Lua scripting replaces VBScript; multi-segment lookahead, deeper cutter comp than LinuxCNC, S-curve options in some plugins. Mach4 + ESS reaches ~4 MHz step rate. **UCCNC** (CNCdrive) pairs Windows software with UC-series motion controllers (UC100 USB to UC400ETH 400 kHz Ethernet); decent for plasma builders. **Planet-CNC** is a closed Mk3/Mk4 USB controller paired with proprietary software, popular for PCB milling.

## Standalone CNC controllers and the jerk-limited motion frontier

**TinyG (Synthetos, 2010)** was the first widely-deployed *hobby* CNC controller to do **constant-jerk (3rd-order) S-curve planning in real time**, on an 8-bit ATxmega192A3 @ 32 MHz with four DRV8818 drivers. Jerk held piecewise-constant gives parabolic velocity during accel/decel phases, avoiding the velocity step a trapezoidal planner produces at corners (and the small-but-real positional ringing). TinyG also pioneered JSON config/status protocol over USB serial — every parameter (`{xvm:5000}`) is JSON-addressable. TinyG was discontinued in favor of g2core.

**g2core is the C++ rewrite for ARM Cortex-M3/M7** (SAM3X8E Due, gQuadratic SAMS70 M7, gQuintic SAMS70N19A @ 300 MHz with FPU). Built on Synthetos's "Motate" submodule (a constexpr-templated pin/peripheral HAL), bare-metal not RTOS, GPLv2 with BeRTOS exception. **g2core advanced from 3rd-order to 6th-order jerk-bounded planning** using **5th-order Bezier velocity curves** producing 6th-order position trajectories. Setting boundary v, a, j to (v_start, 0, 0) and (v_end, 0, 0) gives polynomial coefficients in closed form, making per-segment math cheap enough for Cortex-M3 without FPU stall. Successive derivatives (snap, crackle, pop) are bounded; the practical effect is continuous acceleration AND continuous jerk, which is what the machine body actually feels. **No commercial CNC products advertise that they use 6th-order motion planning.** Cornering uses a **Junction integration Time (JT)** parameter computing non-zero stopping velocities from jerk limits and angle. Step rates: ~200–250 kHz on Due, much higher on SAMS70. The fb:100 release added 3D-printing support (heaters, ABL, planner perf) and a NodeJS comms module (`g2core-api`); the canonical browser UI is **ChiliPeppr** with the Serial Port JSON Server (SPJS) bridging USB-serial to WebSocket.

**Buildbotics** is a CERN-OHL-S open-hardware controller pairing a Linux SBC with an AVR XMEGA-class step generator, 4 stepper outputs at up to 250 kHz/channel, S-curve acceleration, web UI, Onefinity OEM deployment. **Centroid** (Acorn, AcornSix, Allin1DC, Hickory, Oak) is closed-source proprietary using a hybrid design: Windows 10/11 PC running CNC12 software handles UI; **a 1 GHz BeagleBone Green serves as the dedicated motion CPU on Acorn**, communicating over Ethernet. AcornSix adds 6-axis step+dir, dual analog/PWM. Allin1DC integrates 3-axis 15A DC servo drives. Hickory is the new EtherCAT closed-loop platform supporting 8 EtherCAT servos. CNC12 has trapezoidal+S-curve, **rigid tapping**, lathe canned cycles, ATC support, conversational programming (Intercon), step+dir output to drives. Centroid's "P241 sync-up distance" parameter for rigid tapping confirms the standard pattern: Z is geared to actual spindle position, not closed-loop RPM, with sacrificial lead-in degrees while Z accelerates to mate spindle velocity.

**Masso (Hind Technologies, Australia)** is a polished closed-source standalone PC-less controller with HDMI out, USB host ports, Wi-Fi for MASSO-Link file transfer, and integrated 15" touchscreen on the G3-Touch. Hardware is proprietary embedded ARM+FPGA-class. Three software tiers (Essential, Core, Advanced US$490–2090) on identical hardware; Advanced adds **Lua scripting for full automation logic**, real-time event triggers, multi-machine coordination, lights-out workflow recovery. Up to 5-axis; mill, router, lathe, plasma, laser, waterjet profiles. **DDCSV (Shenzhen Digital Dream)** is a Chinese standalone controller built around an ARM9 main CPU + FPGA, embedded Linux. V3.1 does 4-axis at 500 kHz; DDCS-Expert does 5-axis at 1 MHz. ~$200–600. Motion planning is basic trapezoidal (not jerk-bounded). The community **Pandora-CNC** firmware patches add USB MPG support, custom splash screens, and serial console into the underlying Linux.

**BIGTREETECH ships no first-party firmware** but produces hardware running Marlin/Klipper/RRF: SKR Mini E3 V3 (STM32G0 M0+ @ 64 MHz, four onboard TMC2209s), SKR V1.4 (LPC1768), SKR Pro (STM32F407), SKR 3 (STM32H743/H723 M7 @ 480–550 MHz), Manta series with combined Klipper-host MCU + companion-SBC slot (Manta M8P V2.0 has STM32H723 M7 @ 550 MHz plus a board-to-board socket accepting Pi CM4, BTT CB1, or BTT CB2 directly), CB1/CB2 Pi alternatives (Allwinner H616, 1.5 GHz quad A53), and the Pad 7 Klipper appliance. **BTT's commoditization of 32-bit boards killed the AVR market in 2019–2022 and made high-performance motion control accessible at $40–80 boards.**

## Algorithmic and academic foundations

The motion-planning literature is rich and most firmwares draw from a small canon. **Sonny Jeon's junction deviation post (onehossshay.wordpress.com, 24 Sep 2011)** is the foundational hobby-world cornering algorithm. **Bobrow/Dubowsky/Gibson "Time-Optimal Control of Robotic Manipulators Along Specified Paths"** (Int. J. Robotics Research, 1985) and **Shin & McKay "Minimum-time control of robotic manipulators with geometric path constraints"** (IEEE Trans. Auto. Control, 1985) introduced phase-plane bang-bang TOPP. **Pfeiffer & Johanni "A concept for manipulator trajectory planning"** (1987) gave the cleaner numerical-integration version. **Pardo-Castellote & Cannon (Stanford early 1990s)** extended to redundant manipulators. **Verscheure et al. "Time-Optimal Path Tracking for Robots: A Convex Optimization Approach"** (IEEE Trans. Auto. Control, 2009) showed that substituting `b(s) = ṡ²` and `a(s) = s̈` makes TOPP **convex** (an SOCP solvable globally). **Pham & Pham "TOPP-RA"** (IEEE Trans. Robotics, 2018, arXiv:1707.07239) implemented as the open-source `toppra` library uses two passes — backward computes controllable sets via small LPs; forward greedily picks max admissible control. **Grbl's two-pass v² planner is essentially a TOPP special case for kinematic constraints with junction-deviation curvature limit.**

**Robert Ellenberg's circular-blend-arc TP (LinuxCNC, 2014)** builds on local-toolpath-smoothing literature: **Tajima & Sencer "Kinematic corner smoothing for high speed machine tools"** (Int. J. Mach. Tools Manuf., 2016), **Beudaert/Lavernhe/Tournier "5-axis local corner rounding of linear tool path discontinuities"** (Int. J. Mach. Tools Manuf., 2013), and **Tulsyan & Altintas "Local toolpath smoothing for five-axis machine tools"** (Int. J. Mach. Tools Manuf., 2015).

**Higher-order trajectory generation** progresses from trapezoidal (2nd-order, infinite jerk at phase transitions) through 3rd-order constant-jerk 7-segment (TinyG) to **5th-order quintic Bezier** (Marlin's `S_CURVE_ACCELERATION`, Bezier velocity curves of degree 5 with continuous v, a, j at endpoints) and **g2core's 6th-order**. **Lambrechts/Boerlage/Steinbuch "Trajectory planning and feedforward design for electromechanical motion systems"** (Control Engineering Practice, 2005) is the canonical lithography-grade 4th-order reference. **Reflexxes** (Torsten Kröger, ~2010, commercial) was the original real-time online trajectory generator; **Ruckig** (Berscheid & Kröger, "Jerk-limited Real-time Trajectory Generation with Arbitrary Target States", RSS 2021, arXiv:2105.04830) is the open-source successor — first time-optimal OTG with arbitrary multi-DoF target states (including non-zero target acceleration), validated against >10⁹ random trajectories at <1 ms per call. LinuxCNC has experimental Ruckig integration via the `tpmod` module API.

**Step generation algorithms**: Bresenham (1962, IBM Systems Journal 1965) provides integer-only step coordination but suffers follower-axis timing jitter. DDA (per-axis fractional accumulator) gives independent timing at higher bookkeeping cost. **Klipper's iterative solver** uses secant-method root finding (super-linear convergence ~1.6, ~2 iterations per step) on the composed `stepper_position_at_time(t)` function — making new kinematics trivial (just write the forward map) and enabling exact step times. **Step compression** packs constant-acceleration step sequences into `(interval, count, add)` triples where `step[i] = step[0] + interval·i + add·i·(i-1)/2`, with bisection in `compress_bisect_add` finding the longest compatible run. **Sonny Jeon's AMASS** in Grbl uses power-of-2 ISR oversampling preserving Bresenham exactness while distributing follower-axis pulses uniformly.

**Input shaping** originated with **Singer & Seering "Preshaping Command Inputs to Reduce System Vibration"** (ASME JDSMC 112:76-82, March 1990, originally MIT AI Memo 1027, January 1988). The math: given 2nd-order oscillator response and damping ratio ζ, design an impulse sequence `(A_i, t_i)` with Σ A_i = 1 such that residual amplitude after the last impulse is zero. With period `T = 2π/ω_d` and `K = e^{-ζπ/√(1-ζ²)}`, ZV uses two impulses `A₁=1/(1+K), A₂=K/(1+K)` at t=0, T/2; ZVD adds derivative-zero robustness with three impulses; **EI/2-Hump EI/3-Hump EI** (Singhose/Seering/Singer 1994) trade exact-zero for ≤5% residual over wider frequency bands; **MZV** is Klipper's Dmitry Butyugin variant. Convolution implementation `shaped_position(t) = Σ A_i · unshaped_position(t − t_i)` is mathematically equivalent to a sparse FIR filter. Smoothness/robustness ordering: ZV < MZV < ZVD ≈ EI < 2HUMP_EI < 3HUMP_EI. Singhose's PhD thesis "Command Generation for Flexible Systems" (MIT 1997) is the canonical text.

**Pressure advance** descends from Matthew Roberts (Reprap, ~2009) and JKN-Advance (Sailfish), with **Sebastian Popp's LIN_ADVANCE** in Marlin as the canonical hobby implementation. The model: nozzle outlet pressure ≈ k·v_extrude (steady flow), so compensate by `E_compensated(t) = E_planned(t) + K · v_toolhead(t)`. Differentiating, `Ė_comp = Ė + K·a_toolhead`, meaning extruder runs faster during accel and slower during decel. At an acceleration discontinuity the extruder must instantly jump velocity — the same vibration/skipping problem motivating XY input shaping. **Klipper's smooth_time** convolves with a triangular FIR window (default 40 ms) yielding C¹-continuous extruder velocity. **Tronvoll/Popp/Elverum/Welo "Investigating pressure advance algorithms for filament-based melt extrusion additive manufacturing"** (Rapid Prototyping Journal 25(5), 2019) validated LIN_ADVANCE against Simulink dynamic models; K is layer-height-dependent.

**NURBS interpolation** in industrial controls (Siemens 840D, Fanuc 30i, Heidenhain TNC) outputs NURBS rather than densified G1 segments; the controller's interpolator chooses chord density adaptively. Academic references: **Yeh & Hsu "Adaptive-feedrate interpolation for parametric curves with a confined chord error"** (CAD, 2002), **Erkorkmaz & Altintas "Quintic spline interpolation with minimal feed fluctuation"** (J. Manuf. Sci. Eng., 2005), and the canonical **Erkorkmaz & Altintas "High Speed CNC System Design" Parts I–III** (Int. J. Mach. Tools Manuf., 2001).

**Kinematics math**: CoreXY: `A=X+Y, B=X-Y` (and inverse `X=(A+B)/2, Y=(A-B)/2`). Linear delta tower: `h_i = z + √(L² − (x−t_x)² − (y−t_y)²)`. SCARA two-link: `cos(θ₂) = (x²+y²−L₁²−L₂²)/(2L₁L₂)`, two solutions (elbow up/down), singularity at `x²+y² = (L₁±L₂)²`. Stewart hexapod: forward kinematics is iterative (no closed form). Serial-arm 6-DoF: D-H parameters per joint; iterative inverse via Newton-Raphson on the Jacobian, with damped least squares `J⁺_DLS = Jᵀ(JJᵀ + λ²I)⁻¹` near singularities.

## CNC vs 3D printer feature divergence and convergence

The differentiation between 3D printer and CNC motion needs reflects fundamentally different physical regimes: 3D printers have light-mass toolheads, no cutting force, but a coupled extruder axis whose pressurized-polymer physics dominates print quality, with slicers emitting enormous numbers of short G1 segments. CNC has high-mass spindles, large reaction forces, sparse-but-precise CAM-authored toolpaths, with errors dominated by tool deflection, backlash, and thermal growth rather than vibration ringing.

**CNC-specific features absent or marginal in printer firmwares** include G54–G59.3 work coordinate systems (RRF supports; Marlin does not), G43/G43.1/G49 tool length offsets with persistent tool tables, G40/G41/G42 cutter compensation (requiring lookahead through subsequent moves — LinuxCNC's shallow lookahead here is a documented weakness), canned cycles (G73/G81–G89), **G96/G97 constant surface speed** for lathes (requires tight RPM control loop), **G33/G76 threading and G84/G33.1 rigid tapping** (Z motion electronically geared to spindle position via encoder/index — note that precise RPM is *not* required because Z is geared to actual spindle position, so a stalling spindle simply slows Z), multiple spindles for live tooling, **plasma THC** (LinuxCNC PlasmaC reference using External Offsets and Mesa THCAD voltage-to-frequency modules), lathe diameter/radius modes (G7/G8), 5-axis RTCP/TCPM, full Renishaw-style probing (G38.2–G38.5 with edge/corner/bore/boss cycles), tool changers with prep/change separation (T prepares, M6 swaps), spindle orient (M19), real-time override characters (GRBL 0x90–0x9C), and Manual Pulse Generators.

**3D-printer-specific features absent in CNC firmwares** include pressure advance/linear advance, input shaping (though increasingly studied for CNC laser/router surface finish), filament runout detection, power-loss recovery (Prusa's Power Panic uses a hardware comparator on the rectifier triggering emergency state-save before bulk capacitor sag), mesh bed leveling (conceptually equivalent to CNC's external-offset-driven 3D height map), M600 filament change, multi-extruder mixing, MMU integration, hotend thermal models with feedforward, M486 print object cancellation, and adaptive resonance per Z height.

**The distinction is blurring**. High-speed printers borrow CNC techniques (Bambu/Voron-class machines hit 500–800 mm/s with 20,000+ mm/s² accel, limited by frame resonance not planner). CNC routers borrow input shaping for surface finish — academic work (Springer 2018) demonstrates input-shaping-driven corner rounding for short G01 paths is mathematically clean. Hybrid commercial machines (Snapmaker 2.0 with Marlin 2.0 + FreeRTOS in Snapmaker2-Controller) explicitly target FDM + laser + CNC. **Klipper expanding into laser/small CNC and FluidNC's 3D-printing experiments** show that 32-bit ARM SoCs cheap enough to host any firmware are blurring hardware lock-in. The cross-domain firmwares (RepRapFirmware, g2core, increasingly Klipper and FluidNC) treat 3DP, CNC, laser, and plasma as configurations of the same engine — which is the architectural direction of 2026.

## Architectural lessons and recommendations for from-scratch design

The strongest design principles emerging from this survey are about *where to draw abstractions*, not *which algorithms to pick*. Algorithms matter (a 6th-order planner beats trapezoidal; an iterative solver beats Bresenham at high microstepping), but architectures determine whether your firmware can absorb the next decade of changes — closed-loop steppers, EtherCAT, eddy-current probes, hybrid 3DP/CNC machines, ML-driven autotuning. **The firmwares that aged best abstracted *something*; those that aged worst optimized for the cheap MCU of the day.**

**Adopt a host/MCU split if your target machines have reliable 24/7 SBC accompaniment.** Klipper's iterative-solver-plus-step-compression model is the most copy-able modern architecture, with three load-bearing pieces: clock synchronization across multiple MCUs (`clocksync.py`-style frequency estimation), a self-describing protocol (DECL_COMMAND + zlib data dictionary downloaded at connect, eliminating coordinated host/firmware version updates), and bit-packed `(interval, count, add)` step encoding that reduces the MCU hot path to integer adds. The cost is the buffer-underrun cliff: design your scheduling discipline knowing that a host stall will halt the print. If you need standalone reliability for unattended machines, monolithic on an M7 (STM32H7, NXP iMXRT1062 Teensy 4, GD32H7) is viable with care — RepRapFirmware on Duet 3 SAME70 is the proof.

**Make configuration data-driven, not preprocessor-driven.** YAML in flash (FluidNC), TOML, INI/cfg (Klipper, LinuxCNC), or JSON object model (RRF, g2core) — anything but Marlin's monolithic Configuration.h with 100+ KB of conditional compilation. Allow live-edit-then-restart. Marlin's pain is well-documented (issues #5913, #25110); FluidNC users can share machine configs as text files and iterate without a toolchain.

**Expose a HAL even if simple.** A function-pointer table (grblHAL style) is enough — you don't need LinuxCNC's full pin/signal RT graph. But abstract: stepper outputs, IO inputs, spindle interface, temperature inputs, communication channels. The grblHAL approach demonstrates that minimal HAL plus a plugin event/registration system enables extensions (Trinamic, Modbus VFD, plasma THC, encoder, MPG, networking) without forking the core. **LinuxCNC's HAL is the gold-standard architectural reference even if you only adopt 10% of it** — the concept of typed pins (bit/float/s32/u32) connected by named signals running in deterministic threads is the right abstraction for any open-architecture controller.

**Plan for both 3DP and CNC regimes.** Implement G53–G59.3 work coordinates from day one (almost free), G43 tool length offsets, and real-time override characters (GRBL 0x90–0x9C). These open the door to CNC/laser/plasma later. **A well-architected modern firmware should treat 3DP, CNC, laser, and plasma as configurations of the same engine** — which is what RepRapFirmware, g2core, and increasingly Klipper and FluidNC are converging toward.

**Use junction deviation cornering with optional jerk-limited overlay.** Sonny Jeon's algorithm is cheap (no trig, two sqrt per junction), physically grounded, and dominant in modern hobby firmware. For high-end targets, layer a 3rd-order S-curve (TinyG/Synthetos approach) or 5th-order Bezier (Marlin/g2core approach) on top — or integrate Ruckig as the planner core for industrial-grade jerk-limited multi-DoF time-optimal trajectories. **g2core's 6th-order jerk-bounded planner remains the open-source frontier**; the closed-form tractability of 5th-order Bezier velocity curves makes it computable on Cortex-M3 silicon.

**Use Klipper-style iterative solvers for kinematics extensibility.** The secant-method root finder on a composed `stepper_position_at_time(t)` function takes the same CPU as hand-derived inverse formulas while making new kinematics trivial. **Adding a kinematic should require only a forward function** — no algebraic inversion, no segmentation. RepRapFirmware's analytic segment-free delta motion is the alternative high-end approach if you accept per-kinematic mathematical work.

**Implement input shaping as host-side convolution of the position formula** (Klipper approach) — this is the direct payoff of an iterative-solver architecture. With a fixed-time-grid stepper (Marlin's FT_MOTION, Prusa's Precise Stepping), implement shaping in the trajectory generator. Either way, support at minimum ZV, MZV, ZVD, and EI shapers; provide accelerometer-driven autotuning (ADXL345/MPU-9250/LIS2DW) with FFT-based identification.

**Synchronize pressure-advance with input-shaped XY** from day one if extrusion is in scope. Klipper's recent integration is the reference. RepRapFirmware 3.5+ propagates input shaping into pressure advance. Treat extruder dynamics as a first-class kinematic, not a bolted-on afterthought.

**Distributed motion via CAN-FD is a near-term inevitability.** Duet 3 + 1LC architecture is the model: 2-wire bus to the toolhead carrying heater PWM, thermistor, fan, accelerometer, and tool-board MCU. Klipper's CAN support exists but is documented as less mature than RRF's CAN-FD; design protocol versioning carefully. Even if v1 is single-MCU, structure the protocol layer for distribution.

**Self-describing protocols + JSON object model** so UIs don't need firmware-version lock-in. RepRapFirmware's full Object Model exposed over HTTP, Klipper's Moonraker+Mainsail/Fluidd, FluidNC's `$$`/`$Config/...` queries — all converge on this pattern. Your firmware's stable API should be canonical machining functions (LinuxCNC `canon.hh` is the reference) plus a JSON state tree.

**Macro/scripting layer above G-code** — meta-expressions (RRF style with `if/while/var/global` and OM access), or full Python embed (Klipper-via-Moonraker style). Avoid Mach3-style closed VBScript. LinuxCNC's `REMAP` facility (Python prologs/epilogs around G/M codes) is the most flexible model.

**Probing primitives (G38.2–G38.5) and mesh height-map** as a shared substrate — they serve mesh bed leveling on 3DP and edge/center finding on CNC equally. **External Offsets** (LinuxCNC 2.8+) — a HAL pin applying real-time axis position offset outside the trajectory planner — is essential for plasma THC, master/slave following, thermal/warp compensation, and possibly 3DP eddy-current live-mesh. Build it in.

**Plan for closed-loop steppers and skipped-step recovery** — design step counts and physical position as separate variables so encoder feedback can correct. The Mechaduino, BTT S42B, and MKS SERVO ecosystem is becoming default expectation; current firmware integration is shallow because the architecture wasn't designed for it.

**Avoid premature MCU-class optimization.** Marlin's AVR-era integer math, GRBL's 8-bit assumptions, and Mach3's parallel-port realtime all aged poorly. Modern Cortex-M4F/M7 single-precision floats are nearly free. Multi-core M7 (STM32H745) and dual-MCU split architectures are the new baseline. Don't bake bus-width or ISA assumptions into your APIs.

**The unifying narrative of motion control firmware in 2026** is that the underlying problems (smooth real-time trajectory generation, multi-axis coordination, feedback integration, configurable kinematics, distributed I/O) are converging across domains. The differences between 3DP and CNC firmware are domain-specific G-code dialects and feature modules, not core architecture. The next generation of firmwares — whether new projects or major versions of existing ones — will be the ones that internalize this convergence and design for it from the start. **Design for the union, not the intersection.**
